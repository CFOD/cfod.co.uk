The English School - A 14 Day essay

This essay served as a significant milestone in my pursuit of a Master's degree and involved undertaking all research, planning, and writing within a strict 14-day timeframe, intending to assess my progress during Term 1. Additionally, it served as an effective marker of my general ability for both myself and my tutors, following an extended break from writing of just over three years.


What are the core elements of the English School’s approach to IR? How, if at all, does it differ from realism?


This essay will argue the core elements of the English School’s approach to International Relations (IR), how they are based around and emphasise social cooperation, mutual interests and international society, as well as pluralism and historicism, and how these interlace to underpin a coherent, unique school of IR. It demonstrates the distinct perspective the English School offers, comparing and contrasting it to that of Realism, illustrating the nuanced disparities between them in their interpretations and explanations of the international system and general IR. It concludes that the English School is nuanced in its depiction of IR, thanks to the adaptation of and more accurate understanding of Realist theories, alongside historical accounts of world events.


This essay will be using both historical and theoretical data from the 1950s to modern day, starting with that of the inception of the English School via the meeting of the British Committee on the Theory of International Politics, moving to that of current and future International Relations (IR), a medium of which we will apply our comparisons between theories to substantiate our claim. This essay will focus on primary data from those at the centre of the creation of the English School, such as Hedley Bull and Martin Wight, in conjunction with those at the core of the English School's ‘rebirth’ in the early 2000s, such as Barry Buzan. I will then use these in conjunction with modern-day IR theorists and scholars such as Andrew Linklater, Dale Copeland and Jacinta O’Hagan alongside my arguments to substantiate the core elements of the English School, as well as their differences from Realism. It is argued that the English school is not unique, and has failed to break away from the classical themes of IR and political thought (Buzan, 2001, p. 471), however, these are generally disregarded as incorrect (Buzan, 2001, p. 471) and this essay will prove not just how this is incorrect but has the basis of a uniquely historicist, vastly pluralist and unique field of IR.


‘The English School’ is the name given to a school of IR beginning in the late 1950s with the meeting of the British committee on the Theory of International Politics. The English School bases its school of thought in a Constructivist, Pluralist, and Historicist mid-ground between that of Realism and Liberalism (Copeland, 2003, p. 427; True et al., 2013, p. 89; Thompson, 2018, p. 788), and although there have been questions of the boundaries of the English School (Wight, 1960; True et al., 2013, p. 89; Dunne, Kurki and Smith, 2016, p. 108) in that it takes considerable influence from that of Realism, the English School uses and modifies the core tenets of many IR theories to substantiate and explain states’ behaviours pertaining to that of its own theory.

Throughout history, there have been two main critiques of the English School, both relate to each other stating that not only does the school not provide enough clarity of its claims, but that most of its core claims do not differ enough from Realism (Buzan, 2001, p. 471; Copeland, 2003). The English School takes a standpoint that can be seen to be very broad and yet also rather focused, dependent on the literature or point in which the theory is viewed. Both of these claims will be addressed in this essay, however, will be addressed in a manner that shows the differences between the English School and Realism, and not with the goal of attempting to prove or disprove the boundaries within. The English school, alongside the subject of IR, has also faced numerous claims of Eurocentrism and racism within its works (Oskanian, 2013; O’Hagan, 2023), in that the Western/European place in world history has allowed schools of IR to define key terms and binaries such as East and West, North and South, forward and backward and civilised vs uncivilised (O’Hagan, 2023, p. 4) whilst also largely analysing how Western states relate to each other in a system of largely Western actors and organisations, such as the typically ‘western’ fixation on order over justice, and the ‘non-western’ emphasis for global justice (True et al., 2013, p. 100). This is a key issue that needs to be addressed, however, it does not pertain to the definition of the core elements of the English School, nor how they differ from Realism, therefore will be given due consideration, however, not directly addressed in this essay.


Within the English School of IR, two of the key thinkers have described it as having three key and core elements, these being the ‘three traditions’ – Hobbesianism / Machiavellianism, Grotianism and Kantianism (Buzan, 2001, p. 475), and the ‘three elements’ - The international system, the international society, and the world society (Stivachtis, 2018, p. 1). In The Anarchical Society Bull makes early and clear definitions of IR and the components of an international system in that it is comprised of states; that being, groups or communities of peoples in which a government asserts control, both territorially and politically (Bull, 2012, pp. 8–17). An international system, he claims “is formed when two or more states have sufficient contact between them and have sufficient impact on one another’s decisions to cause them to behave” (Bull, 2012, p. 9). Then, according to Bull, a society exists “when a group of states, conscious of certain common interests and common values, form a society in the sense that they conceive themselves to be bound by a common set of rules in their relations with one another” (Watson, 1987, p. 1; True et al., 2013, p. 94). Likewise, Realism, much like the English School, places a significant focus on statehood, with Mearsheimer asserting that ‘Survival depends on a state’s material capabilities, and its alliances with other states’ (Dunne, Kurki and Smith, 2016, p. 36) as well as authors suggesting that the main assumptions of realism are state-centricity; the state as a unitary, rational actor, the notion of power and the emphasis on structure (Fernandes, 1991, p. 42). These are all core of the English School; however, they must be allocated different weighting depending on their significance within the school’s interpretation of IR. Notably, international society is deserving of the highest weighting within the English School, as it is the most crucial pillar in the support of this framework.

The international system and the international society are two highly significant and core elements of the English school, alongside the use of pluralism and history, seeing that without the presence of the international system and the international society, the English School at its core, would not exist. Realism, with academics such as Hobbes, Machiavelli and Mearsheimer providing the basis for its core academia, suggests that the world in which we exist is inherently anarchic and that whilst states do exist, they are doing so via a calculation of self-interest, in an attempt to gain the maximum degree of power, therefore, ensuring their survival (Mearsheimer, 1995; Korab-Karpowicz, 2023). For the international system and international society to exist within the English School, they must not disregard, but build upon these core realist principles of anarchy and self-interest, alongside the pluralist vision of order, in a comprehensive, versatile manner, as it is the absolute existence of order, and therefore a basic international system, which not only allows states to fundamentally exist, (as without order, even the basic state would fall into complete anarchy) but cooperate and interface in even on the most basic level, countering the Realist’s raison d’etat. Whilst the society allows states to further break free from the complete anarchy suggested in The State of Nature, this is, however, not to suggest that the international system is not anarchic, as there is no higher monopoly of power controlling these states, merely mutual interest and social cooperation.

Taking into account Bull’s statement that international order is “A pattern or structure of human relationships that sustain not the special purposes of this or that sort of society but the primary or elementary goals of social coexistence; goals that are common to social life at all time and in all places” (Bull, 1971, p. 270). The presence of international order and therefore an international system must exist, both on a personal and societal level, as humans do coexist, and in a world of multiple states, cultures, traditions and world views, the one core fundamental venture that must be agreed on, is the presence of an international order, as there is no alternative to coexistence and a basic level of order other than continuous warring and societal collapse, such as in Hobbes’ The State of Nature, which could still be argued as ‘an order’, even if not the utopian ideal (True et al., 2013, pp. 89–90). Considering the English School’s, at times, constructivist approach to IR, such as in Wendt’s account of actors being moulded by their normative structures and institutions (Wendt, 1992), as well as a recurring account of power-politics within the English School (Bull, 1971, 2012; Buzan, 2001; True et al., 2013), we can then say that historically, with the concept of states having always existed, be that peacefully or otherwise, order and therefore an international system must be guaranteed. This is where the English School breaks away from and transcends the core concepts of Realism. It does not reject the notion of power politics and statehood focus, nor anarchy within an international system, more so, it expands on and develops this, as common interests, human interaction and order are brought into account as fundamental elements of humanity, and therefore IR.


The English School of IR is strikingly eclectic in regards to its methodologies used when formulating grand arguments in which we confine our world. As mentioned earlier in this essay, there have been claims that the English School has lacked focus in its core ideologies and boundaries concerning IR, however, on the contrary, it should be argued that compared to classical approaches to IR such as Realism or Liberalism, which embrace a greater ‘qualitative vs quantitative’ methodology, the wider reach and at times constructivist and pluralistic analysis of IR is allowing the English School to substantiate a more comprehensive and balanced theory, capable of being applied to any period of history, both in the realm of international and domestic relations. By embracing pluralist tendencies within the English School, we can add further context and support to previously made claims that it is not only a core component of the theory itself, but also allow the school to demonstrate coherence in claims of international society being the natural order in a mid-ground between that of Realism and Liberalism.

At the core of the English School when examined through a pluralistic lens, is the acceptance that the international society is not solely occupied by that of states, but nations, ethnic groups, and more generally, those who have a political voice as a community (Buzan, 2001, p. 476). Taking into account Bull and Watson’s classic definition of International Society (Buzan, 2001, p. 476), it is impossible to view political actors in a social system in the same way one would examine a physical system. For us to effectively examine a political realm within IR, we must use the English School to move past that of the Realist unitary power-politics and incorporate political actors into our fundamental understanding of the development of society. Sentience; that is, being human with the ability to communicate, allows us to better understand the relationships between political actors. If states, actors, nations or groups within an international system establish even limited communication routes and a basic understanding of common norms and values, they can allow themselves boundaries of behaviour concerning their neighbours. As political entities have leaders, usually representing the people within, and these leaders are sentient with the ability to communicate and therefore not only understand the norms and values of their neighbours but express their own values, they can be said to implicitly shape and define the international society in which they occupy (Buzan, 2001, p. 477), just as human beings as individuals shape the domestic society around them.

As stated earlier, the key and core concepts of the English School revolve around that of the international system and international society, and to achieve a comprehensive account and defence of this, the English School has also adopted historical and therefore pluralist views on IR. By using the historical and pluralist viewpoints of IR within the English School, we can examine the formation of the system to discern the mechanism by which international societies are formed. History can be seen to be used by many core English School scholars, such as Wight and Bull (True et al., 2013, p. 94) in the examination of the formation of international society. Wight used the history of many well-documented ancient civilisations to establish his theories of international societies forming in the presence of high-level linguistic and cultural unity. By using this interconnection of core values, he claimed that states felt they were connected under a superior civilisation (True et al., 2013, p. 94). Bull also observed that “ideas of human justice historically proceed the development of ideas of interstate or international justice and provided perhaps the principal intellectual foundations upon which these latter ideas first rested”. Consequently, it seems that both Wight and Bull believe that inter-state communication and cultural unity are fundamental before the development of an international society. Without the use of this historical, pluralist viewpoint under the English School’s lens, the English School would not be able to postulate upon historical events regarding the formation of international society, in a manner effective enough to break free from its realist foundations, whilst establishing its place within the modern schools of IR.


In conclusion, The English School of IR provides a unique, well-rounded and multi-disciplinary approach to IR, as well as understanding the international system. By scrutinizing the core elements of the English School, we can better understand how it allows us to gain a complex and varied insight into IR. This essay has demonstrated the need to understand the distinctions between the international system vs the international society, as these are core components of the English School, whilst, chiefly, being core components of humanity and sentience in itself. For us to arrive at this conclusion, this essay has also argued the need for the historicist and pluralist viewpoints adopted by the English School, as the ability to look back on prior historical events and attribute them to our contemporary world, grants us the capacity to understand our place in IR with utmost efficiency. Limitations of this argument could be said to be the potential reliance on Realist theories to underpin the teachings of the English School, however, this is not a critique of the English School, as it does not use these theories in themselves, but takes, transforms and transcends past these to create nuance and veracity.
