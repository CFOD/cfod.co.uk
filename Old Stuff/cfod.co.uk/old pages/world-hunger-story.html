World Hunger Test Page

I feel the answer to this question can be answered both globally and in regard to the United States specifically. From some very rough calculations, around 1 in 10 people are experiencing hunger, or do not get enough to eat. Likewise, it seems around 700-800 million people are facing chronic hunger. Considering this, Oxfam America claims that it would take around $14m a year, for the next 6-7 years to end chronic world hunger sustainably. On a global scale, this would mean roughly doubling the current commitments of foreign aid for a number of years, but when put into US-specific terms, the numbers become even more upsetting.


An aircraft such as the F35 Lighting II, for example, is one of the US government's most expensive and ambitious military endeavours to date. In comparison to the figures above, the around $332 billion spent to date on a technological advancement aimed towards the destruction of others could end chronic world hunger over two times. Adding to this, a United States Government Accountability Office Report to Congressional Committees stated that it may cost around $1tn to develop, maintain and upkeep these aircraft. Although a commitment to end world hunger is and should be a worldwide effort from developing nations, given the UN estimate of $267bn a year for the next 6 years to completely end world hunger altogether, should the US commit the same resources to this as it did one single military advancement, they could almost pay for this in entirety, alone.
